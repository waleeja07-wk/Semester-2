Expository Writing
Definition

Expository writing is a type of writing that aims to explain, inform, or describe a topic clearly and objectively using facts, evidence, and logical organisation — without expressing personal opinion.

🗂 Types of Expository Writing — Quick Map
📰
Informative Writing
Provides facts and information about a topic without bias. Answers What? Who? Where? When?
Example topic
"The water cycle and its stages"
💡
Explanatory Writing
Explains how or why something works. Breaks down complex ideas into understandable parts.
Example topic
"How inflation affects the economy"
🖼️
Descriptive Writing
Creates a vivid picture of a person, place, or thing using specific sensory details.
Example topic
"Describe the Grand Bazaar of Istanbul"
🔢
Process Writing
Explains a procedure step-by-step. Clear sequence is essential. Answers How is it done?
Example topic
"How to conduct a scientific experiment"
✏️ Key Features of Expository Writing
  • Objective tone — no personal opinions ("I think", "I believe")
  • Uses facts, statistics, examples as evidence
  • Clear, logical structure (intro → body → conclusion)
  • Third-person point of view
  • Precise, formal vocabulary
🎯 Practice Questions
  • What is the difference between informative and explanatory writing?
  • Write an informative paragraph about climate change (no opinions!).
  • Identify which type: "The Nile River flows 6,650 km through northeastern Africa." → Informative

Essay Writing & Article Writing
📐 Essay Structure Flowchart
Thesis Statement

A single sentence that states the main argument or point of an essay. It appears at the end of the introduction and guides the entire essay.

✅ Strong Thesis Example

"Social media has transformed political activism by enabling rapid mobilisation, broadening participation, and amplifying marginalised voices — though it also poses serious risks of misinformation."

Article Analysis Writing — WHAT, HOW, WHY

What is the article about? → How does the writer develop their ideas? → Why is this effective/ineffective? Always support with evidence from the text.

📌 Article vs Essay — Key Differences
  • Essay: Academic, formal, thesis-driven, no subheadings usually
  • Article: Publication-ready, can have subheadings, audience-aware, often includes quotes from sources
  • Both require: clear structure, evidence, and coherent argument
🎯 Practice Questions
  • Write a thesis statement on: "The impact of technology on education"
  • Analyse: How does the writer use statistics in this article? What effect does this create?
  • What makes a topic sentence effective? Write one for a body paragraph on pollution.

Rhetorical Appeals
Definition

Rhetorical appeals are strategies used by writers and speakers to persuade an audience. Aristotle identified three primary appeals: Ethos, Pathos, and Logos.

E
Ethos
Appeal to credibility. Establishes the author's authority or trustworthiness.
P
Pathos
Appeal to emotion. Connects with the audience's feelings and values.
L
Logos
Appeal to logic. Uses facts, data, and reasoned argument.
✅ Examples in Context

Ethos: "As a doctor with 20 years of experience, I can confirm that vaccination is safe." — appeals to professional authority.

Pathos: "Every year, thousands of children go to sleep hungry. Can we really afford to ignore them?" — evokes guilt and empathy.

Logos: "Studies show that countries with universal healthcare have 15% lower mortality rates." — uses data and reasoning.

🔺 Rhetorical Triangle
🎯 Practice Questions
  • Identify the rhetorical appeal: "Nine out of ten dentists recommend this toothpaste."
  • Write one sentence using Pathos on the topic of environmental destruction.
  • Why is it important to balance all three appeals in writing?

Reading Skills — Skimming & Scanning
⚡
Skimming
Reading quickly to get the general idea / main topic of a text. You don't read every word.
How to skim
Read title, headings, first & last sentences of paragraphs, bold words.
When to use
Deciding if an article is relevant; previewing a textbook chapter.
🔍
Scanning
Searching for a specific piece of information (name, date, statistic) without reading everything.
How to scan
Let your eyes dart across the page looking for keywords, numbers, or specific terms.
When to use
Finding a phone number in a directory; locating a fact in a report.
⚡ Memory Trick

SKIM = General → like skimming cream off milk (get the top layer)
SCAN = Specific → like a barcode scanner (looking for one exact thing)

🎯 Practice Questions
  • You need to find the year a treaty was signed. Skim or scan?
  • You want to know if a 20-page report is about climate policy. Skim or scan?
  • List three strategies you use when skimming a newspaper article.

Photo Essay & Picture Description
Photo Essay

A collection of photographs arranged with accompanying text to tell a story or convey a message. Each photo contributes to the central theme.

🖼 Picture Description Framework — WHAT · HOW · WHY
✅ Sample Picture Description

WHAT: The image shows an elderly woman sitting alone on a park bench, surrounded by falling autumn leaves.
HOW: The photographer uses a shallow depth of field, blurring the background to isolate the subject. The muted, golden colour palette enhances the melancholic atmosphere.
WHY: This creates a sense of loneliness and the passage of time, inviting the viewer to reflect on aging and solitude.

🎯 Practice Questions
  • What is the difference between a photo essay and a series of random photos?
  • How does lighting affect the mood of a photograph?
  • Practice: Look at any photo and write a WHAT-HOW-WHY analysis in 3 paragraphs.

Interview Skills
💼 Interview Structure Flowchart
🗣
Interview Techniques
Open questions: Invite detailed answers ("Tell me about...")
Closed questions: Yes/No answers — use sparingly
Probing: "Can you give an example?"
Active listening: Nodding, paraphrasing back
Silence: Allow the interviewee time to think
🎭
Role Play Dialogues
In role play, adopt the character's voice and purpose.

Key elements: Appropriate register (formal/informal), turn-taking, staying in character, responding naturally to cues.
Tip
Prepare opening, 4–5 core questions, and a polite closing.
🎯 Practice Questions
  • Write 5 open-ended questions for an interview with a scientist about climate change.
  • Convert this closed question to open: "Do you like your job?" → ?
  • Write a role-play dialogue: journalist interviewing a climate activist (10 exchanges).

Research Methodology
🔬 Research Paper Structure
📌 Key Definitions
  • Literature Review: A survey of existing research relevant to your topic — shows what is already known and where gaps exist.
  • Methodology: The systematic approach used to collect and analyse data (qualitative/quantitative, surveys, experiments, etc.).
  • Bibliography: Alphabetical list of all sources used, formatted according to a citation style (APA, MLA, etc.).
✅ Good Research Title Examples

❌ Weak: "About Climate Change"
✅ Strong: "The Impact of Industrial Emissions on Urban Air Quality in Pakistan: A Case Study of Lahore (2018–2023)"

🎯 Practice Questions
  • What is the purpose of a Literature Review?
  • Write a research title on: "Social media and mental health in teenagers"
  • Explain the difference between qualitative and quantitative methodology.

Report Writing
Definition

A report is a structured document that presents information about a specific topic, event, or issue to a specific audience for a specific purpose — informing, analysing, or recommending.

📋 Standard Report Structure
  • Title / Heading: Clear label (To, From, Date, Subject)
  • Introduction: Purpose and scope of the report
  • Findings: Organised information with subheadings
  • Conclusion: Summary of key points
  • Recommendations: (if required) What should be done
✅ Report Header Example

TO: The Principal
FROM: The Student Council
DATE: 19 May 2026
SUBJECT: Report on the Annual Science Fair

🎯 Practice Questions
  • Write the header and introduction for a report on: "Canteen facilities at the university"
  • How does a report differ from an essay? (Think: structure, purpose, audience)
  • Write two recommendations for improving library facilities.

Letter Writing
Formal Letter
Written to officials, businesses, institutions. Uses formal language, full addresses, specific structure.
Uses
Job application, complaint, request to authority, official inquiry
Informal Letter
Written to friends or family. Conversational tone, flexible structure, personal language.
Uses
Writing to a friend, personal updates, casual requests
📍 Formal Letter Structure

Your Address (top right)
Date

Recipient's Name & Address (left)

Dear Mr/Ms [Surname], ← Salutation

Subject: [Clear one-line subject]

[Opening paragraph — state purpose]
[Body — details, evidence, explanation]
[Closing paragraph — call to action or request]

Yours sincerely, ← (if you know the name)
Yours faithfully, ← (if you wrote "Dear Sir/Madam")
[Signature + Printed Name]
⚡ Golden Rules
  • "Dear Sir/Madam" → always end with "Yours faithfully"
  • "Dear Mr Smith" → always end with "Yours sincerely"
  • Formal letter: no contractions (don't → do not, can't → cannot)
  • Every letter needs a clear purpose stated in the first paragraph
🎯 Practice Questions
  • Write a formal letter to your university principal requesting an extension on an assignment.
  • Correct this closing: "Dear Sir, ... Yours sincerely" → What's wrong?
  • What are three differences between a formal and an informal letter?

Rhetorical Devices — Purpose & Tone
Definition

Rhetorical devices are literary techniques used to strengthen a writer's argument, create a specific effect, or appeal to the audience. They shape both purpose and tone.

Repetition & Emphasis
  • Anaphora: Repeating a word/phrase at the start of successive sentences — "We shall fight... We shall never surrender."
  • Alliteration: Repetition of consonant sounds — "Peter Piper picked..."
Comparison Devices
  • Simile: Comparison using "like" or "as" — "brave as a lion"
  • Metaphor: Direct comparison — "Life is a journey"
  • Analogy: Extended comparison to explain — "The brain is like a computer"
Questions & Engagement
  • Rhetorical question: Asked for effect, not answer — "Can we afford to wait?"
  • Hypophora: Raises a question then immediately answers it
Tone-Shaping Devices
  • Irony: Saying the opposite of what is meant
  • Hyperbole: Deliberate exaggeration — "I've told you a million times"
  • Understatement: Downplaying something significant
🎯 Identifying Device → Effect → Purpose
✅ Full Analysis Example

Sentence: "Our planet is a patient with a fever — and we are the disease."

Device: Metaphor — the planet is compared to a sick patient.
Effect: Creates a sense of urgency and guilt; makes the abstract (climate change) feel personal and immediate.
Purpose: To persuade the audience to take climate change seriously by positioning humans as the cause.

🎯 Practice Questions
  • Identify the device and explain its effect: "Shall we surrender? No — we shall fight!"
  • Write one sentence using hyperbole to describe exam stress.
  • What is the difference between a simile and a metaphor? Give one example of each.
